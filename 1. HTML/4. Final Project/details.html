Somewhere, Some Place

They call it Somewhere. No one recalls who named it that, or if it ever had another name. It's the kind of place you stumble upon rather than arrive at—the roads seem to lead there on their own accord, winding in ways that maps don't account for. The air hums differently here, neither warm nor cold, as though it hasn't quite decided what it wants to be.

The skyline blurs, an uneven mix of hills that might be mountains and buildings that might be trees. Shadows stretch longer than they should, casting shapes that seem vaguely familiar but refuse to settle into anything recognizable. Locals—if they can be called that—drift through the cobbled streets with knowing smiles, their eyes holding stories they won't tell. Shops open and close without pattern; signs swing in breezes that never quite touch your skin.

In one corner, a clock tower ticks backward, or perhaps sideways—no one bothers to check. Somewhere's market smells faintly of spices you've never tasted but swear you've known forever. Lanterns sway above narrow alleys that widen when you're not looking, leading to plazas that weren't there the day before.

You might hear music—or laughter—from behind a door that won't open again if you leave. And there's a bridge, or maybe a pier, that vanishes into a fog too thin to be real. People say it leads to Elsewhere, though no one agrees on where that is.

Stay long enough, and you might find yourself remembering things you've never done. Or was it dreaming? But that doesn't matter much here. In Somewhere, everything feels on the verge of happening, or perhaps has already happened—it's hard to say.

And yet, you stay. Because in Somewhere, leaving feels a bit like forgetting—and who would want that?
